Solve for the spectrum you want

Draw a target spectrum — the tones you intend to hit — and least-squares fit the n phase rotations whose reachable spectrum best approximates it. With phase-only knobs you can place one sharp line anywhere and tune its width; multiple separated peaks need amplitude shaping and stay out of reach. The fit shows you exactly how close finite qubits get.

Recovering the signal from a spectrum is the inverse transform — but you only get to pick the power |V[k]|², not its phase, so a whole family of signals shares one target (phase retrieval). Your spins realize the flat-magnitude member, shaping only the phase θ(x)=Σφⱼxⱼ shown here. A single line has a flat-magnitude member, so it's reachable; broad or multi-peak targets don't — their signals demand varying magnitude, which needs amplitude (Ry) control, not just phase.

The math why one tone, and only one
Reachable family — flat magnitude, bit-additive phase
$$P(k)=\prod_{j=0}^{n-1}\cos^2\!\Bigl(\tfrac{\varphi_j-2\pi k\,2^{\,j}/N}{2}\Bigr)$$
Exact single tone — any frequency inverts in closed form
$$\varphi_j=\frac{2\pi k^\star}{N}\,2^{\,j}\pmod{2\pi}\;\Rightarrow\;\text{line at }k^\star,\ \text{width}\approx\tfrac1N$$
Closed-form gradient — drives the fit
$$\frac{\partial P}{\partial\varphi_j}=-\tan\!\Bigl(\tfrac{\psi_j}{2}\Bigr)P,\qquad \psi_j=\varphi_j-\tfrac{2\pi k\,2^{\,j}}{N}$$
The wall — two tones beat in magnitude
$$e^{iax}+e^{ibx}=2\cos\!\Bigl(\tfrac{a-b}{2}x\Bigr)e^{i\frac{a+b}{2}x}$$
